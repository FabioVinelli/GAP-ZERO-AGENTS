SUMMARY
Forrester analyst Craig Le Clair explains why AI agent deployments fail to deliver ROI and prescribes measuring, then closing, the gap between insight and action.

IDEAS
Only fifteen percent of enterprises reach measurable ROI territory from their many AI agent deployments today.
High ambition without execution creates POC purgatory, where pilots multiply but business benefits never actually materialize.
The agentic action gap measures distance between an agent's insight and a resulting value-driving business action.
Friction counts unintegrated systems and humans required to execute insights; manual activity quietly kills agent ROI.
Time between prediction and remedial human action determines whether insights ever translate into real economic value.
Acceptance of recommendations without modification is a trust metric revealing how truly implementable agent outputs are.
Injecting AI into processes and expecting good results automatically is fiction; outcomes require deliberate workflow redesign.
Humans today function as middleware, manually bridging gaps between agent insights and the actual execution systems.
Future operating models flip roles: agents orchestrate execution while humans become endpoints pinged only when needed.
Reports and dashboards represent failure modes; interfaces should be redesigned to actively de-emphasize passive information display.
Volume metrics like tickets closed should yield to measures of decision complexity and genuine decision quality.
Waiting in stagnation has one upside: learning cheaply from other organizations' early costly mistakes and misjudgments.
Transactional efficiency on narrow repetitive patterns delivers speed but stops far short of operating model transformation.
C-level pressure drives pilot proliferation without ensuring pilots connect to the material patterns of daily work.
Thirty pilots can generate zero ROI when agents merely produce dense reports that nobody acts upon.
Orchestration layers register, supervise, and coordinate agents, providing policy enforcement, real-time observability, and full lifecycle control.
Automation platforms become the digital hands of agents, clicking buttons and triggering APIs in legacy systems.
Human roles shift from multisystem task execution toward knowledge curation, agent oversight, and critical thinking skills.
Some revised models let agents execute everything, pausing only at the final step for human review.
Application-embedded prebuilt agents win when good enough, inheriting execution capability from their surrounding domain workflow platforms.
Nearly half of decision-makers demand majority returns on AI investments before ever declaring those investments successful.
Measuring friction points lets organizations prioritize specific process locations for digitization and then further agent-led automation.
Track differences between agents' potential value and actual business outcomes, isolating exactly where execution breakdowns occurred.
Agents may be workflow peers or prime controllers; either way, map and convert every manual step.
Cognitive observability remains immature today, limiting meaningful scrutiny of agent behavior, but improvement is coming soon.
Most successful deployments remain agentish rather than agentic, well positioned for future self-learning and tool creation.
Adaptive process orchestration platforms combine RPA, integration services, and low-code into one unified agent execution infrastructure.
INSIGHTS
Intelligence without actuation is economically worthless; value lives entirely in the connection between knowing and doing.
Organizations unconsciously assume insight naturally converts to action, ignoring that conversion itself is the scarce capability.
The report presumes legacy system integration, not model intelligence, is now the binding constraint on value.
Framing humans as middleware quietly redefines people as friction, an assumption with profound unexamined organizational consequences.
Measurement precedes management: gaps invisible to metrics remain politically deniable and therefore never get systematically closed.
Pilot proliferation functions as organizational theater, satisfying executive pressure while deferring the harder work of redesign.
Trust is operationalized as unmodified compliance, implicitly assuming agent recommendations deserve acceptance rather than human skepticism.
Technology adoption failures are usually workflow design failures; tools rarely fail, but their surrounding processes do.
The unstated bet is that agent errors cost less than the human latency they would eliminate.
Speed metrics reward motion; quality metrics reward judgment; organizations get whichever behavior their measurements actually celebrate.
Blindspot: accountability, liability, and error recovery when autonomous agents act wrongly receive essentially no treatment here.
Humans reduced to pingable endpoints may lose the contextual expertise that made their oversight valuable initially.

HABITS
Assess your agentic action gap before investing any further money in additional pilots or proof-of-concept deployments.
Count friction points regularly: every unintegrated system or human handoff needed to execute each agent insight.
Measure time elapsed between every agent insight and the corresponding human action that actually delivers value.
Track the percentage of agent recommendations that human workers accept and execute completely without any modification.
Select use cases with clearly defined business outcomes before deploying any agents into live production workflows.
Map each manual step causing friction, then convert those steps into one reliable fully automated workflow.
Redesign user interfaces deliberately to de-emphasize reports and complex dashboards that merely invite passive information consumption.
Adopt KPIs emphasizing decision quality over velocity, replacing volume counts like tickets closed or calls made.
Deploy custom agentic frameworks capable of multistep iterative planning rather than relying on simple single prompts.
Design agents to orchestrate internal action sequences from goals, not merely suggest possibilities to overloaded humans.
Introduce a management layer registering, supervising, and coordinating agents with policy enforcement and full lifecycle control.
Monitor both vendor-native and third-party agents continuously through unified cognitive observability and real-time lifecycle monitoring capabilities.
Run agent experience skills programs preparing employees for knowledge curation, change management, and ongoing agent oversight.
Let agents execute everything until the final step, then pause and present results for human review.
Isolate execution breakdowns by comparing each agent's expected potential value against the actual measured business outcome.
Prioritize digitizing specific process points where measured friction concentrates before attempting any broader agent-led automation initiatives.

RECOMMENDATIONS
Measure friction, time to action, and compliance before funding any additional new AI agent proof-of-concept projects.
Kill pilots producing only reports; redirect budget toward agents wired directly into actual execution systems instead.
Choose one use case with a clear business outcome rather than launching thirty scattered simultaneous pilots.
Before flipping to agent-centric models, define clear accountability structures for moments when autonomous agents act incorrectly.
Question whether high compliance reflects genuine trust or merely automation bias and unexamined human rubber-stamping.
Preserve human contextual expertise deliberately; endpoints pinged occasionally lose the judgment that oversight roles genuinely require.
Retrain displaced workers into knowledge curation, critical thinking, and agent oversight roles before restructuring entire workflows.
Replace dashboard-heavy interfaces with action-oriented designs that trigger workflows instead of merely displaying passive static information.
Adopt orchestration layers with policy enforcement and real-time observability before granting agents direct execution authority anywhere.
Measure decision quality and complexity rather than raw volume metrics that reward motion over meaningful judgment.
Start with application-embedded agents in domain-centric workflows whenever good enough clearly beats building custom framework complexity.
Track expected value against realized business outcomes continuously, isolating precisely where each execution breakdown actually occurs.
Pilot fully autonomous execution on reversible, low-stakes processes first before touching any irreversible high-consequence business workflows.
Budget explicitly for integration and process redesign, not just models; integration is the actual binding bottleneck.
Audit whether eliminating human latency quietly creates new risks exceeding the delay costs it removed originally.
Use stagnation strategically if resources are scarce, studying competitors' expensive early mistakes before committing serious capital.
Establish security and governance guardrails before agents click buttons and trigger APIs in critical legacy systems.
